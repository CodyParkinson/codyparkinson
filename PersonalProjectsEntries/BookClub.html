01/08/2024

After completing a semester of school, it can be difficult to gauge just home much you have learned. You can get A’s in everything, but how can you know if you actually ‘know’ it? This book took away all of my doubt, and I could not recommend it enough to those interested in what it takes to run a nuclear reactor (pressurized water reactor (PWR) to be precise).

How to Drive a Nuclear Reactor, is an overview of the engineering, physics, and chemistry that goes into creating power from glowing rocks. There are about 450 operating nuclear power reactors worldwide, most being PWRs, so this is the focus of the book. The book is funny, witty, and just very easy to follow. Of course there is a lot of ‘slang’ and acronyms within the nuclear world, but Colin Tucker describes each one thoroughly and often restates what they were if it had been a few chapters since the term was introduced.

This next part I am about to write is a big ole pat on the back to myself, so if you don’t want to read the following narcissism, stop here. But, the classes I finished last semester were Nuclear Engineering Basics, Nuclear and Energy Systems, and Reliability and Risk Assessment. I am going to have a page describing each of these classes and what I learned in my journal entries, so go there if interested in what those classes entailed. Here, I am going to relate these classes to the book:

Nuclear Engineering Basics: this class is the fundamentals of how we take an atom and get energy out of it. The first sections of this book talk about physics including cross sections, thermalization of neutrons, and decay pathways. This was the whole point of the class; how do we take an atom, like Uranium, and get it to fission? As I read I couldn’t help but be amazed how I could describe these processes and that I really feel I have an understanding of them. Albeit, I am currently enrolled in Reactor Physics, so I still have lots to learn about geometries and transport theory, I feel as of right now I am solid in my nuclear basics.

Nuclear and Energy Systems: I loved this class. I would consider this my pathway into an understanding of thermodynamics and engineering design. Every day I learned something new, and this was the bulk of this book. When you look at the design of an energy plant, whether it be coal, gas, nuclear, there are key structures you need: a power source (Qin), turbine (Wout), cooler (Qout), and a compressor (Win). This is a very simplistic view, but it holds for a continuously cycled PWR. This is just how you generate power to the grid, and each of these can be broken down into thousands of subcomponents. For example, in a nuclear power plant, your power source is the reactor vessel. This requires a vast amount of components. Considering this is a PWR and not a boiling water reactor (BWR), the reactor core has its own coolant water circulating. This coolant water must transfer its heat to another system containing water (steam generator). The coolant water and steam water cannot be allowed to mix. And on and on and on.

Reliability and Risk Assessment: So you have these thousands of different pieces, systems, and components. What if one breaks? What if you have a loss of coolant? We must have systems in place to ensure we do not have a meltdown. In the book it is described that all PWRs must be able to withstand a large break, double guillotine, LOCA. This is exactly what was taught in the course. To counteract this we must have an emergency core coolant system. This system needs to be diverse, duplicated, and physically separated. In most plants, there are four backups. For example, a plant would have four backup diesel generators. This allows one to be down for maintenance, one to fail unexpectedly, and two to run (only one is needed, so if two happen to break you’re still okay). This is the only point of complaint in the book. Colin Tucker states that plants must be able to handle ‘Credible events’, this is a deterministic design, and it is ineffective. I believe in fault tree analysis or chain of events. It is the idea that small events are what lead to large events (see Three Mile Island). Looking to Wash-1400, it states that deterministic design is not all encompassing; there are many more factors which need to be considered than just ‘credible’ events.

This is my hopefully not too long report of the book, me just describing how smart I feel. Jokes aside, this is an excellent book. I have loved every minute of my degree thus far, and this book only helped to strengthen that love further. Wish me luck.

It has been a minute since I finished a book. The last few months have been pretty hectic with school, so it was nice to take a bit and read a fun book over the Thanksgiving holiday. I’ve been thinking about reading this book for a while now, and I don’t know what happened but I’ve had this urge to revisit old MTV shows. I rewatched a couple episodes of Rob and Big, Rob Dyradek’s Fantasy factory, Teen Mom, Cribs, Jersey Shore, Wildboyz, and of course Jackass. Over the course of a few hours, I had this hit of nostalgia. Growing up it was cool to be a part of the skateboarding crew and wear Plan B t-shirts; I remember watching the Art of Flight over and over and drinking the old flavor of Rockstar mango (the new one doesn’t compare to how it used to taste). I don’t think really anyone can pinpoint the day when they just sort of stopped doing something, and this is how I think about this time. I don’t know what happened, but I just sort of stopped being into those things and became interested in other hobbies. What I do know is the transition was into programming and computers, so quite a leap.

What has never left me, was the crazy stunts of MTV and especially one star: Steve-O. This guy’s stunts are etched on my brain. I ended up rewatching the first two movies, and it just blows me away the things he was willing to do (which is a major theme of the book). I have listened to quite a few of his podcasts with guests that I was interested in like Nyjah Houston, Johnny Knoxville, Chris Pontius, and just other crazy folk. This all led me to the penultimate decision to finally just read his book.

Two things I love about this book, first, I just love a good autobiography. Second, it's fun to read about someone’s life that I will just never live (nor have the desire to). Steve-O grew up as a ‘corporate brat’. Like a military brat, but with more money and a father in big business. He spent most of his childhood living in Europe and moved to Florida to attend university. As you can imagine from his later years, he did not do so well academically. In fact, I think he said he ended up with a 0.7 GPA overall (although one semester when he did apply himself he did get something like a 3.0). From here the story is he moved around some more, had some odd jobs, went to Barnum and Bailey Clown College (which is statistically harder to get into than Harvard), and eventually became a famous movie star.

Probably not the best summary of his life, but I’m not so interested in just giving a summary of the book. What I am interested in is more of the life he lived off camera and how he justified his lifestyle. Early on in the book he discusses his mother’s alcoholism and how hidden it was from his father. He and his sister would watch their mom succumb to this deep depression and self soothing through alcohol, but when their father was home from a business trip she was able to snap herself out of it. She would clean herself up and pretend nothing had happened. I thought about this a lot while reading. This is how Steve-O seemed to live his life. If he got loaded on drugs or alcohol, as long as he was able to portray himself as ‘okay’, that was all that mattered.

Eventually his mother and father split up, and she ended up with major health complications. She needed round the clock care, and it ultimately became Steve-O’s sister’s job to care for her. He states that he couldn’t really even look at her; it just made him feel too bad. So, he left, and he justified his leaving by wanting to make his mom proud and becoming successful. Ultimately towards the end of his drug use days, he talks about how he never took the time to let go of this goal or let go of his mother’s eventual passing, and this led to thoughts of self-harm and other problems. While reading, you get this sense that Steve-O is the most dedicated person alive. There is no one else willing to put their body at risk like he was willing to. There is a very genuine feel to the book. I have no doubt that he was as dedicated to fulfilling his promise of being a successful stuntman. Sounds like a cliche, but he really did live his life, in this really messed up way, for others, especially his mom. I think without even realizing it, he decided to show his respects to his mom the only way he knew how. He knew he couldn’t take care of her physically, which call it selfish to leave it all up to his sister, but that is how his brain handled it.

Steve-O did become sober in 2008, and he published this book in 2011. It is unfathomable with the amount of drugs, alcohol, partying, stunts, and everything else that he is in as good of shape as he is. Since 2011, from listening to his podcast, it sounds like he is still doing well and is 15 years sober. In this silly way, it was sad to read this book. When I was young, I was very naive. I didn’t understand drugs. I thought Steve-O and the crew did the stunts just because they loved doing it. In part, this is probably somewhat truthful, but once you read about how the network paid them nearly nothing and the rampant drug use, it doesn’t have the same charm. Yeah dude, what an interesting guy. Well, back to school and getting through finals.

I have never seen the movie, so I decided I better read the book before I do. I like to think of myself as a Jack Nicholson fan, and Taylor tells me One Flew Over the Cuckoo’s Nest is definitely one of his best movies. So, I picked up the book and just read it.

Oh boy, did it deliver. This is very cliche and sounds like a bad newspaper review, but it was thought provoking and absolutely captivating. You move around this very small world that is confined to a mental asylum, from the view of a Native American from the Dalles in Oregon. For most of the book Chief, the Native American, doesn’t talk, so it is like reading a third person account. Everyone thinks he is deaf and dumb, so he gets to see and hear everything. No one questions having in the room sweeping while they discuss confidential material. As the book progresses, there is this crescendo where he finally breaks and starts talking. The book then shifts to this first person view as the chief takes back his personal sovereignty.

The thing is though, the book is not about the Chief. It is about McMurphy (Jack Nicholson in the movie). This absolute breakout force that tries to change the way the asylum is run. This battle ensues around Nurse Ratched and McMurphy, and that is ultimately the story of the book. McMurphy is this larger than life, pseudo cowboy that can’t be confined by rules; Nurse Ratched is an army nurse who runs the unit like clockwork.

Without spoiling too much, you could say that neither of them win. Both of them strip each other of their identity. But, I would like to make an argument that I haven’t been able to find anything on. If you look up what others write about the book, you will find things about how the book addresses racism, sexism, disability, shame, and on and on. These are most definitely present, and they all deserve their own papers. I don’t have the time to do that, nor the want. What I am most interested in is this sad reality of how this was just the way it was done. Right or wrong, I could believe that those working, nurses, orderlies, the doctor, were doing their very best. They were not rooting for electroshock therapy because they were sadistic, but they ultimately thought this was for their, the patient’s, best.

This makes the viewpoint of the Chief a very interesting aspect of this book. He was self admitted to the hospital, and in the end was able to check himself out. This begs the question then, can we rely on this perspective? Clearly he was not blind and dumb, and there is explanation in the book about how this came to be through his childhood. This, in my mind though, was not enough of an explanation to keep this act up for a decade. I have to wonder whether or not he really did have a mental disability. The way he described the ward being covered in a fog, the mechanisms in the walls, the robotic ways of people, I would believe that he needs help.

This leads into my argument about the perception of the Chief and that of the whole book. Can we trust him? He most definitely has a right to be frustrated and upset by those around him. He was cast from his home by white people wanting to build a dam on the river, and not until he was on the ward did he really have experiences with people of other races. He very easily could have projected his frustration onto these people, even though they had nothing to do with the changing of his homeland. Add all of this on top of his paranoia, and I would call this an altered perception.

This all makes me wonder, were the orderlies that rough with the patient’s? Was Nurse Ratched that mean spirited of a lady; a lady who was ready to bulldoze anyone who got in her way? Was the doctor this dumb, pushover of a man? I don’t know. I think about the time period, the 1950’s. Lobotomies were seen as a miracle of science, the first waves of psychoactive drugs were coming through, electroshock therapy was in use; these things were not used as torture. They were used because this is what was available. In hindsight, we can look back and say it was wrong, but we could make that argument for a lot of things. I have this thought about what I would be thinking if I was a nurse ,and I was taking care of McMurphy. McMurphy was a true disturbance. He repeatedly broke ward policies, and he just seemed to be irate. He would threaten staff, and his threats seemed credible given his actions. I would be thinking, how could we help him become better?

McMurphy builds this relationship with people in the ward, and he gets them to turn on the nurses and orderlies. Nowhere in the book is he described as not being a conman. In fact, he is repeatedly referred to as such. He uses people and substances for his gain. His past is checked, and from this view, I definitely do not view him as a hero of the ward. Someone who comes in and shakes things up for the better. Even in the end of the book, you see this destruction he leaves in his wake. McMurhpy does help a few of the patient’s ‘escape’, but it really isn’t escape. The only one who you feel like has a chance is Chief Bromden, but you have to ask yourself whether or not his freedom was worth the death and destruction of so many others. This could also lead into a whole other argument about that, but I digress.

Yeah, I like writing after finishing a book, because it just helps get my thoughts sorted out. I really loved this book, but I feel quite torn about it. Good news is, I can finally go watch the movie.

07/09/2023

This book kind of came out of nowhere for me. I was perusing the YouTube when I saw that The Minimalists had posted their documentary on there. I had this faint recollection of watching it when it first came out, like maybe six-ish years ago. I am always fascinated by niche cultures like this one, and after watching the documentary again, I decided to look into their books. They have written quite a few, so I just kind of picked one at random, and it just happened to be Everything That Remains. I do not know where this book falls in their storyline, but it was short. So I thought, why not?

I think the way I think about life is not different from most people. Some days you think a very basic, minimalist lifestyle is the key to happiness, and other days, you think opulence is the key to happiness. This is something that I have thought about quite a bit, and I can never quite decide which one is better. What I usually come back to is, maybe it’s somewhere in the middle.

This book is a memoir, so mostly just the history of how Joshua and Ryan become The Minimalists. The very brief synopsis is, they both worked in the corporate world until their late twenties when they realized that something was wrong. They were working too much, their relationships were failing, they had too much debt, and they were just overall unhappy. Josh met a world traveler who lived out a duffle bag, and he decided instead of world traveling, he was just going to learn how to live with less. His thought process was, if this guy could live out of a duffle bag, he could easily pair down his belongings to a more manageable amount. He got his friend Ryan in on his plan, and the rest is history. This is a very important point, because I do believe it does “normalize” them a bit. They aren’t these monks who only own a pair of sandals and a robe; they were, and still are, just people.

Sprinkled between the history are the lessons they learned along the way, so I am just going to summarize some of the ideas that stuck out to me. The first one is a longer quote, but one that I really enjoy, “I’ve always claimed that my priorities are grandly important activities like spending time with family or exercising or carving out enough alone time to write. But they’re not. Until I actually put these pursuits first, until I make these undertakings part of my everyday routine, they are not my actual priorities.”I reread this a few times, and I just kind of shook my head at it. I hate to say it, but I really felt this one hit home. How many times do I say something is important to me, but yet, I waste time instead of actively pursuing it? Without getting too deep into my personal examples, I just think this is something I need to work on. Really check myself. Why am I wasting time on things I don’t like doing, things that do not bring me happiness, when I could be pursuing things that bring passion to my life? I don’t know, but I am looking to find out and make the necessary adjustments.

Chapter five, “Strong Moves Slow”, hits the point hard about the shifting of the mindset to every possession should serve a purpose. What I like most about this idea, though, is it is not only about our physical items. Does the food you eat serve you? Do your relationships? Your work? If not, they should be cut out. This is the biggest misconception about these guys, and I felt that reading any of their books is very important before committing to the “minimalist” lifestyle. Everyone thinks minimalism is about throwing out all of your possessions and only owning a shirt, a pair of pants, toothbrush, one fork, one spoon, and one plate. Now some people do this of course, but this isn’t their point. They advocate separating yourself from things with no value. If you love art, they want you to collect and maintain the art you love. This does not mean going to T.J. Maxx and buying a bunch of art just because your walls are bare. Buy art you love; curate with passion. I think we all know what this means. People get upset and throw out all of their sentimental items thinking that that is true minimalism. Don’t do this. There may be a time in which you are ready to relinquish some of these items, but if they bring you joy you don’t have to detach from them. I’ll take a page from Marie Kondo: if it does not spark joy, it does not belong in your life.

This is a small note from the next chapter, “The Sound of Minimalism”, about the dreaded “just in case”. We don’t get rid of things “just in case” we might need it later. But if you haven't worn that shirt or read that book in two years, are you really going to miss it? If you read The Psychology of Money, you would have read Morgan Housel's thoughts on everything having a cost. Even the items that are just laying around. Just a small thought.

Towards the end of the book, there were some ideas that felt strangely “Goggins-esque”. In a definitely less harsh tone, Joshua describes his life without goals. How is this like Goggins at all? Isn’t Goggins the master of reaching his goals? I believe if you sat both of them down in a room together and had them discuss the matter of goals, they would say the same thing: perform without purpose. I believe they would both agree that life is chaotic to be rigid. You have to train yourself to be ready for whatever happens to come your way. It isn’t about goals, it is about putting in the work everyday and trying to be better. Without goals, though, if you don’t happen to finish what you were doing you aren’t sad about it; you know you were better than when you started and you move on. Here is an example, if Joshua was writing a book and ended up scraping it, he didn’t get depressed or put himself down. The book was not a goal. He just wanted to be a better writer, and he became a better writer by putting in the time daily. For Goggins, it isn’t about training for races or events, that isn’t the goal. He just wants to be the best possible person. Now you could argue that being a better writer or a better person are goals, but I see it as more abstract. When I think of goals, I think of hard numbers, places, things. I want to hit this marker, in this place, at this time, and get this thing. Their ideas have no end, they can’t end. You can never “finish” writing. You can never “finish” being a better person. You can only keep putting in work.

The last idea I wrote down comes from this quote, “What you’re imagining is a meaningful life. Not a perfect life, not even an easy life, but a simple one.” Choose your hard. Living a simple life may not be easy, but I think it can be meaningful. Choosing a career that ignites passion, giving up things in order to have time to spend with others, downsizing to minimize debts, these things are hard and intimidating. But even if hard, I believe the otherside can bring immense satisfaction.

Kind of crazy, I didn’t think I had this much to say about this book. It is only about 200ish pages, so very short. With all of this said, I have one major critique of this book; they use too many big words. It is like reading a thesaurus. I don’t think they ever reuse an adjective or verb in this whole book. I mean they use the word uxorious. Do you know what that means? I definitely did not. I guess if this is my only critique, I can still give it a thumbs up.

Do I consider myself a minimalist? Depends. Am I going to change my life and relinquish most of my “unnecessary” possessions? Probably not. Do Taylor and I live in a moderate home and live a moderate lifestyle? Very much so. Give and takes. Like I said in the first paragraph, I am always fascinated by these kinds of people, and I am so glad to have taken the time to learn more about the lives of minimalists.

I have finally finished this book. Funny enough, it is almost the exact same amount of pages as the entirety of The Lord of the Rings (when you also count the extra readings which maybe I did skip some of). I am not a huge history buff; I would consider myself more of a history respecter. I believe in the usefulness of understanding history and how it pertains to the contemporary world. But, I generally do not seek out historical accounts (besides biographies, I do love a good biography). I especially am not well versed in war. The question is then, Cody, how come you read a thousand pages on what the cover of the book looks like an account of World War II?

This book came at the recommendation of Dr. Pope at Idaho State University. One of the big motivations to moving back to Idaho was to continue my education and get my master’s degree. I was looking through programs, and ISU’s Nuclear Engineering program really took my interest. I ended up sending a cold email to Dr. Pope, who answered almost instantly and asked if I wanted to have a meeting with him. A few days later we went over my resume, and I felt like this was something that I could thrive in and really enjoy. I spent a few more weeks researching what I would need to do, what the job market looks like, and where ultimately I might have to end up moving with Taylor. My guts told me this is what my next step in life should be, so I applied. I am happy to say I was accepted, and I will be starting this fall! In the meantime, I am trying to prepare myself for the trials ahead. Biochemistry is definitely different from nuclear engineering, so I need to give myself any edge I can.

So back to the book, Dr. Pope highly recommended this as a way to learn the history of what nuclear engineering looks like. How we went from really no idea what an atom was to harnessing the powers within it. I enjoyed it very much. I took my time reading it, and I am glad I did because it is jammed packed. Richard Rhodes knew how much info he needed to portray, and he wasted no space.

The first quarter of the book reminds me of the first chapter of any general chemistry 101 textbook. Except in this version, it’s 250 pages. The first chapter of these textbooks start with a historical account of chemistry, what an atom is, how we figured out what an atom looks like, who discovered the original constants, etc. The first quarter of this book is exactly this, all the names you have ever heard in chemistry: Rutherford, Szilard, Bohr, Germi, Einstein, etc., etc., and their contributions. What’s really interesting, though, is seeing the struggle each of them faced. For example, when I think of Einstein, I think of packed lecture halls and hours spent at his desk. This is not the whole story. Einstein had to be smuggled out of Germany, moved from place to place, was somewhat barred from the Manhattan Project, and did a lot of his thinking while out on his walks. You forget how nuanced people’s lives can be, and Rhodes did a phenomenal job really showing what these great scientists' lives were really like.

This book has quite a large section on World War 1; how chemists and physicists created many compounds for the war. The creation of poison gasses being some of the most famous. What is most interesting to me, though, is during this time, Bohr and other scientists’ goal was to keep science as open as possible. Nothing studied should be kept under lock and key. The only way for science to move forward is through collaboration and questioning of each other’s work. This is a really big point of the book, because it was a very large point of contention during the creation of the atomic bomb due to its secrecy.

Let me backup a bit. Before there was a bomb, there were thoughts and equations that showed large amounts of energy could potentially be removed out of the splitting of atoms. The problem was, no one knew how to harness that energy. There were theories on how to create self propelling chemical reactions, but no one could figure out how to get atoms to do this. The idea was, if you could get an atom to split off a particle, like a neutron, that neutron could then hit another atom, making it lose a neutron, and this cycle could continue on and on. Szilard (and a lot of Fermi) and his nuclear chain reactions would be one of the greatest stepping stones towards the future bomb.

After its discovery, it was decided that this should be kept secret. The math showed that this potential chain reaction could produce amounts of energy unlike anything the world had ever seen. This was also in the time between WWI and WWII. Europe was shaky and no one really knew what was going to happen next. America and Britain were worried that Germany may have access to scientists who could build a bomb, and so began the nuclear arms race. Don’t forget though, this was not as easy as just keeping a secret. The pushback from scientists in the field was immense, and the contention was highly palpable. It really came down to governments forcing scientists to keep it secret. This, of course, made many angry, and the cycle of contempt continued.

I am skipping over massive amounts of content and history from the book, so if you want more detail, go read the book. But, what I will say is this: there were not that many people who believed this bomb could be created. Think about it, this technology was so new, and really, it was not well understood. Even many of those directly working on the project thought it would be a failure. This made progress slow, but there was always this thought in the back of the minds that if Japan or Germany figured it out first, the war would be lost. This kept people motivated and the money flowing. As you would expect, this program was expensive. Obtaining rare materials, building out new labs, paying all these people to work, required tremendous amounts of capital. But as I said, The price of losing this race to a bomb was worth any cost.

I was thinking I was going to write a bunch more on the historical accounts, but I don’t think that was really the point of the book for me. The history is important, but the impact it had on everyone’s lives from then to now is what I find to be most important. One of the reasons Japan was bombed was due to the morphing thoughts on what Japanese people were to Americans. Americans witnessed kamikaze bombings, brutal fighting tactics, stealthiness in jungle environments, and Americans began to view the Japanese more like animals than humans. There is a line in the book about how they could never have used it in Germany, because the Germans were too much like Americans or the British. They were white people with families; they were just misguided by Hitler and Nazism. The Japanese were seen as brutal for no reason, just animalistic. Although the capital and other major Japanese landmarks were ultimately spared, the decision to bomb Japan was not fought very hard by military or government strategists. Rhodes describes all of this, but he leaves a large portion of the final chapter to accounts of the aftermath. Those that weren’t killed instantly had severe burns and melted skin, people miles away were affected by radiation poisoning, land was unsalvageable due to vaporization, and the list goes on. It was almost like we as America knew that the bomb would be devastating, but not until post-war when individuals traveled to Japan did anyone realize how egregious the aftermath really was.

This leads me to my final thoughts; the point of reading this book, for me, is to realize what can happen if we don’t take the power of nuclear energy seriously enough. We here politicians today talk about nuclear arsenals around the world. We hear of the Middle East, Russia, China, etc. and what they could do if they were to launch a weapon. The problem is, then what? The destruction caused by just the two bombings in Japan is tremendous; the land is unusable and anyone not killed directly deals with the radiation poisoning for the rest of their lives. Generations of land and people gone in a second. Pandora's box of nuclear power has been opened, and its weaponization will remain with us until the end of humankind.

This, to me, is a sad thought, but also one in which I feel like there is opportunity. My biggest goal now is to help be a part of a group that is actively trying to use our knowledge of atomic energy to better the lives of everyone. Instead of weapons, figure out how to make nuclear power more safe, clean, and effective. Find new ways of dealing with waste more efficiently; heck, figure out how to make fusion work. Almost eliminate waste entirely. Stop the destruction of natural landscapes for coal and other fuels. This is my soap box, and I am just truly excited to learn more. In the end, a phenomenal book. One that feels like it will stay with me and will continue to make me think.

I think there is a pretty straight line that could be drawn between most of the books I read, so it can be easy to see why I finally decided to pick this one up. You look just about anywhere for book recommendations, and this one always seems to pop up along the way. After reading, I can definitely see why. There are a lot of good recommendations, practices, and overall life lessons to take out; I just don’t know why I didn’t like it.

The book is written in a fashion that I really like. The chapters have a very descriptive title, and you go in knowing what you should be taking out of it. The chapter starts out with a military story from either Jocko Willink or Leif Babin. This story gives a battlefield account of how they used the chapter's ‘point’ to win. The story is then followed up with an in depth description of the principal the story is teaching. The chapter ends with a business world example of how they used the principal with their company, Echelon Front. This way of storytelling is quite good, and it is such a great way of making sure everyone can understand what is being taught. Also where it is done in triplicate, you can’t say that a topic wasn’t covered thoroughly.

This is a leadership book. There are a lot of topics that can be applied, and really should be applied, to everyday life, but most of them are geared towards those in a leadership position. This is really apparent when you look at some of the major lines of the book like, “there are no bad teams, only bad leaders.” Or even the title of chapter ten, “Leading Up and Down the Chain of Command.” I think this is why I didn’t like the book. I feel I am not yet in a position to completely resonate with the teachings. We all have leadership opportunities in our lives, even when we don’t realize it. If we all take a moment, we can think of things that we are in charge of, people who rely on us, or situations in which we took a main role. But, I feel like I am just not there yet. I feel that I have so much yet to learn about how I operate as a person that it does feel hard to really add these teachings to my life effectively. Coming into the new year, I felt it was a good idea to reacquaint myself with some of the thoughts from this book. It has been over a year since I read it last, but I think about it often. It fits a category that I am not surprised hasn’t been filled (everyone wants the get rich quick scheme), but one that I feel is pretty important.

Do you just kind of know that you are not going to be ‘rich’? Well I kinda do (crying, crying), but it doesn’t make me too sad. The chapters are laid out as general big ideas of money. The book is very easy to read and digest, as it feels like it was written for people, like me, who have never been in a finance class.

“The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving.” This quote comes from chapter 3, “Never Enough”. As the book progresses, Housel gives you the sense that it really is easy to become comfortably wealthy. The trick is, you can’t let your finances get away from you. Once they had something, they just looked for the next thing. He later backs this point up with the notion that, “there are many things never worth risking, no matter the potential gain.” There are things in life that shouldn’t be chanced, and these things look different to everyone. The rule of thumb is if it will ruin you if it fails, it isn’t worth it. This isn’t to say you shouldn’t take risks, but think about what will happen if it were to all fall apart. Housel says something along the lines of, planning is great, but plan on your plan not going to plan.

There is one chapter in this book that I felt really hit home for me, 14 - “You’ll Change.” It is simply about accepting the fact that we will change. It is so simple, but one that I feel very few people embrace. He talks about how some of the most miserable people he has met are people who are loyal to a career just because that is what they decided to major in at 18. Allowing yourself to realize that you are unhappy in a career and being able to move on is essential to being comfortable. “The trick is to accept the reality of change and move on as soon as possible.” Sunk cost fallacy is everywhere in our lives. We believe because we have put something in, we have to get something out. This just isn’t the case. You are not guaranteed anything, so you might as well enjoy the process and motions of what you are doing. This slightly contradicts the idea of compounding, or letting things grow. The book really hammers the point of not touching your investments. Investments not just being stocks, but also careers and relationships. He gives a decent explanation of this dichotomy, but really it boils down to finding a middle ground. Maybe this could have been described better, but it is so nuanced that it could probably have a book of its own written about it.

And maybe that’s the problem. It isn’t that the book doesn’t describe these points, but that broad strokes are given. I frustrate myself by reading books about psychology, because as you would see in my thoughts on Grit, psychology has no set values or ideas. It is a science of ebb and flow. If you say something is true, someone could find multiple examples of it being false. I mean, on the very last page there is a legal notice that Housel or the publisher is not liable if the advice given doesn’t work. I understand that everyone has to cover themselves, but it feels like they themselves don’t believe in it fully; they realize that everything could be different in the future (which funny enough is a chapter in the book). Now don’t let this come across as a bash on the thoughts of the book, for the most part, I feel that they are very sound. It would just be nice to see a discussion on the point of - this book is not infallible.

I’m looking through all of my notes I took on this book, and I am struggling to put together a way to condense all of the final wisdom into just a few paragraphs. So, I am not even going to try. What I will say about the book is this: there are those who are satisfied and those who are not. Morgan Housel describes strategies that would help take someone from the unsatisfied category and help them achieve the latter. Avoiding extreme ends of financial planning, being too frugal or too risky, is key to success. “Wealth is hidden. It’s income not spent.” There are many people who look wealthy but are so leveraged that all of their time is already sold. This is not where you want to be; you want control over your time and life. Just don’t look at this book as a guide to making money. It really is a book about how to keep the money you already have (or will have). I feel it's a good 4.3/5. I recommend it, and I hope to see another publication by Housel in the future.

11/11/2022

I will admit that this book was a grind to get through, but you shouldn’t have it any other way. The book is not easy to read, and there are a lot of terms and names thrown around constantly. Not every line is profound, in fact, most pages are not profound, but as you continue to push, you feel the genius in what he is describing. In a way it feels like I shouldn’t even try to describe the book, because I know I am going to butcher the ideas he is trying to get across. So, take all of this with a grain of salt, as I try to describe all the notes I took.

In the first half, Alan Watts describes the background and history of Zen. With this comes lots of names and terms that we just are never really exposed to in Western thought. Sprinkled in with the history, are ideas that sprung up between the different Zen, Tao, and Buddhist thoughts. Some of the most intriguing I came across was, firstly, the idea of just going with the wind. What is described as a very Western thought are “perfect states”. We think of items, places, people at their most perfect state, and we are constantly seeking this. Watts describes letting go of this and allowing ourselves to not fight change and to see the beauty in what comes next. This leads to a later problem described as the “Western double-binds”. Worry about worrying which leads to more worry. An infinite loop that leads to no good outcomes. In the second half of the book, on teachings, it is thought that you should commit to and move forward. The double-bind can be broken by just choosing a path. Nothing will be perfect, so why try? Move onto what makes you satisfied and pursue. The trick is, you can’t look back once you start. Once you have chosen a path, you must push forward. You are allowed to reflect on what you have chosen, but you cannot reflect on reflecting. This creates worry which leads to the bind.

The second idea I’ve thought about a lot since starting the book, you don’t have to justify your life. Western thought demeans those who are “wasting time”. I will admit that in more modern times, and in most self-help books, meditation is a growing trend. Many people prescribe some sort of meditation. This meditation usually is a five minute routine sitting on the floor thinking about and planning your day. I don’t think Watts would argue that this isn’t a form of meditation, but I think he would say this isn’t really what it was meant for. I think he would also say that we still despise those who are wasteful with time. Meditation is allowing yourself to be justified in what you are doing. Because you were born, because you are alive, you are justified. You do not have to fulfill every dream or be unbelievably successful to be justified. You only have to be. “To the Taoist mentality, the aimless empty life does not suggest anything depressing. On the contrary, it suggests the freedom of clouds and mountain streams, wandering nowhere, of flowers in impenetrable canyons, beautiful for no one to see, and of surf forever washing the sand, to no end.” A very difficult idea to swallow. How could no aim, no meaning, lead to anything good? This finally leads to the ideas of satisfaction duality. What is good is only good in relation to what is bad. A quiet empty life compared to one of pain and insatisfaction. It can be thought of as “the higher you are, the harder you fall.” Allowing yourself the freedom to just move with the wind and be free, can lead to a great fulfilment.

None of this is to say you should do nothing. This is not the point at all. Meditation and fulfillment are usually seen as sitting on a mountain side, cross legged, for years on end, but meditation is whatever you make it. If you enjoy your work, your hobbies, family, all of this is meditation - allowing yourself to just be: justified. Allow yourself to enjoy what you do, even if you don’t feel like it is beneficial to society. “Muddy water is best cleared by leaving it alone,” allow yourself to not stir on indecision and become anxious. Be calm and enjoy the process.

Finally, this is an idea that I am not quite sure on, so you may want to take this with extra grains of salt. I couldn’t quite figure out if everyone is Buddha or anyone can become Buddha. “Those who do know do not speak. Those who speak do not know.” By calling yourself Buddha you have broken the idea of Buddha. Being silent and being allows you to be Buddha. This is a common theme of the book, not allowing yourself to seek but allow yourself to ride the waves. There is no past or future, and the present is self-contained and quiescent. “The flowers depart when we hate to lose them; the weeds arrive while we hate to watch them grow.” In the moments that come, there will be good and bad times, but in those moments remembering your justification and allowing yourself to move with the flow of space.

Trying to surmise the book in a single paragraph would be impossible. Just as there are so many schools of thought in Christianity, so are there in Eastern thought. In this, there is a thought that took me the most. Zen is not about being impulsive and lacking in morality and reflection. Zen is the elimination of blocking of thought and action. Indecision is the killer of fulfillment. Indecision leads to anxiety, but “what comes through the gate is not family treasure.” Much of Zen must be learned on your own. You can’t be told what Zen is; you must live it. I don’t feel like giving this book a rating, but I am glad I found it. In the final pages of the book, Alan Watts reminds, “It’s just this, and nothing else,” so why worry?
